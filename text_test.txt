
The old railway station had been abandoned for nearly four decades, yet it still clung to the edge of town like a forgotten bookmark in a novel nobody read anymore. Elias pushed open the rusted iron gate, its hinges shrieking in protest. Inside, the vast waiting hall was a cathedral of decay. Shafts of pale afternoon light slanted through shattered skylights, illuminating motes of dust that drifted lazily, undisturbed by human breath for years. The floor was a mosaic of broken tiles, scattered leaves, and the unmistakable graffiti of rain. A faded timetable still hung crookedly on the far wall, advertising departures to cities that had since changed their names. "Hard to believe this place used to hum with voices," Elias murmured, his words swallowed instantly by the heavy silence.

He wasn't sure why he had come. Perhaps it was the argument with his brother that morning, a sharp, bitter exchange about selling their grandmother's house. *You can't hold onto everything just because it's old,* his brother had said, pragmatic as always. But Elias felt a strange kinship with these forgotten structures. They were repositories of unspoken stories, of hellos and goodbyes that had simply evaporated into the air. He walked toward the platform, stepping over a rusted bicycle wheel and a single, remarkably intact leather glove. The tracks were gone, reclaimed by wiry weeds and stubborn wildflowers. A crow cawed from the skeletal roof truss above, its voice echoing sharply.

For a moment, Elias closed his eyes and tried to conjure the past: the hiss of steam, the urgent clang of the bell, the frantic embraces. He imagined his grandmother as a young woman, standing right here in a crisp dress, waiting for a letter or a lover. The thought provided a strange comfort, a quiet defiance against his brother's relentless logic. This place wasn't empty; it was simply full of a different kind of life. A life measured in rust and resilience, not in commuter schedules. Opening his eyes, he noticed a small, stubborn sapling pushing through a crack in the concrete platform. It was absurdly green, impossibly hopeful. Elias smiled faintly, a small truce forming in his own mind. Some things were worth keeping, even if they didn't go anywhere anymore. He turned away, the crunch of his footsteps the only sound as the station resumed its long, patient vigil.
